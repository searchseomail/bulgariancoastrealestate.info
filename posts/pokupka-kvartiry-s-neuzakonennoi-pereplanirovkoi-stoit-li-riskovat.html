Покупка квартиры с неузаконенной перепланировкой: стоит ли рисковать?

Sarah Davis 03.04.2026 • Reading time: 6 min

Is Buying an Apartment with Unauthorized Alterations Worth the Risk?

When searching for a new home, you might stumble upon a property that checks all your boxes: perfect location, reasonable price, and a stunning view. However, there is a catch—the floor plan doesn't match the official technical passport. Many potential buyers find themselves asking: "Покупка квартиры с неузаконенной перепланировкой. Кто сталкивался?" This is a common dilemma, and navigating the legal landscape requires more than just a gut feeling. Understanding the difference between minor aesthetic changes and structural violations is the first step in assessing whether this investment is a hidden gem or a financial trap.

Before you commit to a purchase, it is vital to perform thorough due diligence. If you are unfamiliar with the process, I recommend reviewing our complete checklist for verifying a secondary market apartment to ensure you aren't overlooking other critical legal or technical aspects of the property.

Types of Alterations: What Is Acceptable?

Not all unauthorized changes are created equal. Local building authorities typically categorize modifications into two groups: those that can be easily legalized and those that are strictly prohibited due to safety concerns. If a previous owner merely moved a non-load-bearing partition wall, the process is relatively straightforward. However, if they have interfered with the building’s structural integrity or utility lines, you are looking at a much more significant problem.

"The danger in purchasing a property with unauthorized renovations lies not just in the potential fines, but in the structural risks that may compromise the safety of the entire building. Always prioritize technical compliance over aesthetic appeal."

The Risks of Ignoring Unauthorized Changes

If you decide to proceed with the purchase, you must be aware that the legal responsibility for the unauthorized work shifts to you the moment you take ownership. The risks are manifold:

  • Financial liability: You may be required to pay for the legalization process or, in the worst-case scenario, the court may order you to restore the apartment to its original state at your own expense.
  • Mortgage denial: If you are planning to use a loan, banks are extremely cautious. Most mortgage lenders will refuse to finance a property where the floor plan does not match the cadastral documentation. If you are still weighing your options between financing or renting, check out our comparison on mortgages versus renting in current market conditions.
  • Utility issues: Unauthorized changes to heating or water supply systems can lead to conflicts with neighbors and management companies, potentially leading to service disconnection.
  • Resale difficulty: When you decide to sell the property in the future, you will face the exact same hurdles you are experiencing now, likely limiting your pool of buyers.

How to Assess the Situation Before Buying

If you are still interested in the property, hire a professional engineer or a specialized lawyer to inspect the unit. Compare the physical state of the apartment against the documents provided by the seller. Ask the seller directly: "Have you ever attempted to legalize these changes?" If they claim it is "easy" but haven't done it themselves, be wary. Often, the reason it remains unauthorized is that the changes are illegal and cannot be approved under current building codes.

Furthermore, consider the location of the apartment within the building. For instance, if you are looking at a unit that has undergone renovations, ensure the structural integrity remains intact, especially if you are considering buying an apartment on the first or top floor, where issues with dampness or roof access can be exacerbated by poor renovation choices.

Is It Ever Worth It?

Buying an apartment with unauthorized renovations can be a strategic move only if you negotiate a significant discount that covers the cost of legalization or restoration. If the renovations are minor and comply with safety standards, you can use this as a bargaining tool to lower the purchase price. However, never rely on verbal promises from the seller that "everything will be fine." Always consult with local authorities or a professional architect to confirm whether the specific changes can be officially sanctioned.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I buy an apartment with unauthorized changes using a mortgage?
Most banks will not approve a mortgage for a property with significant unauthorized changes. You would typically need to restore the property to its original state or legalize the changes before the bank will issue the loan.
What happens if I don't legalize the changes?
You risk being fined, forced to restore the layout, or facing difficulties when you decide to sell the property or pass it on through inheritance.
Who is responsible for the illegal renovation?
Once you sign the purchase agreement, you become the legal owner and assume full responsibility for all existing conditions in the apartment, including any illegal renovations performed by previous owners.
How can I find out if a wall is load-bearing?
You should request the building's floor plan from the technical inventory bureau. An architect or professional inspector can verify the structural role of any wall during an on-site visit.
