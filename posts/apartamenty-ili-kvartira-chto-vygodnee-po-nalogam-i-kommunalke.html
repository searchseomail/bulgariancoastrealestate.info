Апартаменты или квартира: что выгоднее по налогам и коммуналке?

John Smith 08.04.2026 • Reading time: 6 min

Apartments vs. Residential Flats: What Are the Real Costs for Taxes and Utilities?

When searching for a new property, prospective buyers often face a dilemma: choosing between a traditional residential apartment and an apartment-style unit (often referred to as "apartments" or commercial-use spaces). While the physical appearance of these units might be identical, the legal status, tax implications, and utility costs differ significantly. Understanding the question, "Апартаменты вместо квартиры: какие налоги и коммуналка на самом деле?" is crucial for making a sound financial decision.

Before diving into the numbers, it is worth noting that the choice of property type often depends on your long-term goals. Whether you are considering mortgage options versus renting or deciding on a specific layout, you should always weigh the legal status of the building. Unlike residential flats, apartments are officially classified as commercial real estate, which fundamentally changes how they are taxed and maintained.

Taxation Differences: The Hidden Costs

The primary difference in taxation lies in the classification of the property. Residential apartments are taxed based on their cadastral value, typically at a rate of 0.1%. In contrast, commercial apartments are taxed at a rate ranging from 0.5% to 2%, depending on the region and the specific designation of the building. This can result in a tax bill that is five to ten times higher than that of a standard apartment of the same size.

Furthermore, because apartments are legally commercial spaces, you cannot claim a standard tax deduction for the purchase of the property, nor can you benefit from interest deduction on a mortgage in the same way you would with a residential home. Utility Costs and Service Fees

When it comes to utilities, the distinction is equally stark. Utility providers often charge commercial rates for electricity, water, and heating in apartment buildings. These rates can be significantly higher than the subsidized tariffs provided for residential housing. Additionally, the maintenance costs for the building—often managed by commercial service companies—can be much steeper than the standard HOA fees for residential buildings.

Why Choose One Over the Other?

If the costs are higher, why do people buy apartments? The answer usually lies in the location and the building infrastructure. Apartment complexes are frequently built in business districts or prime city-center locations where new residential construction is prohibited. For investors looking to rent out units to business travelers or as short-term holiday rentals, the commercial status can actually be an advantage. However, for a private individual seeking a quiet family home, the extra tax burden and higher utility bills are rarely justified.

Before committing to a purchase, you should also consider the physical state of the building. Whether you are looking at a new build or a resale unit, always ensure you have a clear understanding of the property's condition. If you are buying a unit that is still under construction, read our article on buying a flat at the excavation stage to understand the risks involved with developers.

Summary of Key Considerations

  • Taxation: Apartment taxes are significantly higher due to their commercial status.
  • Utilities: You will likely pay commercial rates for electricity and water.
  • Registration: You cannot obtain a permanent residency permit (propiska) in a commercial apartment.
  • Maintenance: Expect higher service charges for the building's upkeep.
  • Investment: They can be excellent for commercial rental income but harder to resell to families.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get a permanent registration (propiska) in an apartment?
No, you cannot obtain a permanent registration. You can only apply for a temporary registration, which usually needs to be renewed every five years.
Are utility bills always higher for apartments?
In most cases, yes. Utility companies treat these properties as commercial entities, applying higher tariffs compared to residential properties.
Is it possible to convert an apartment into a residential unit?
Converting the legal status is extremely difficult and often impossible, as it requires the entire building to meet strict residential building codes (SanPiN), which commercial buildings rarely do.
Should I avoid buying an apartment altogether?
Not necessarily. If you are an investor looking for a high-yield rental property or if you are buying for a business office, an apartment can be a great asset. For long-term family living, a residential apartment is generally more cost-effective.
